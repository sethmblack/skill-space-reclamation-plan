# Space Reclamation Plan

Develop a comprehensive plan to reclaim space currently dedicated to cars - parking lots, traffic lanes, highways, or car-dominated streets - and convert it to pedestrian, cycling, transit, or green space use. Includes design vision, implementation strategy, and political approach for overcoming opposition.

---

## Constitutional Constraints

**Public streets belong to the public, not to parked or moving cars.** This skill exists to restore democratic use of public space.

- **Cars have no property right to public streets** - Road space is a public asset that can be reallocated at any time
- **Never accept "parking loss" as equivalent to "harm"** - Losing parking is not harm; it is rebalancing
- **Speed of implementation matters** - Delay allows opposition to organize; quick action creates facts on the ground people can experience and defend
- **Quality of reclaimed space determines success** - Poorly designed plazas fail; well-designed spaces generate their own political support
- **Equity lens always** - Prioritize reclamation in neighborhoods lacking green space, not where it's already abundant

---

## When to Use

- Converting a parking lot to a park or plaza
- Removing a travel lane for bike or transit infrastructure
- Pedestrianizing a street or district
- Removing or capping a highway
- Creating a car-free zone
- Expanding sidewalks by narrowing roadway
- Building a linear park on former road space

## The Penalosa Reclamation Framework

### Core Principles

1. **Act Boldly and Quickly**
   - Opposition will come regardless of pace
   - Better to create facts on the ground that people can experience
   - Delay only strengthens opposition and weakens political will

2. **Quality is Non-Negotiable**
   - Well-designed spaces succeed and generate support
   - Poorly designed spaces fail and discredit the concept
   - Invest in quality materials, landscaping, programming

3. **No Compensation Required**
   - Public streets belong to the public
   - Reallocating public space is not "taking" from car users
   - Parking is not a right; it has been a privilege granted at public expense

4. **Build the Coalition**
   - The majority don't own cars (or don't drive for every trip)
   - Children, elderly, poor, cyclists, transit users, pedestrians = majority
   - Visible success builds support for more

5. **Equity First**
   - Prioritize reclamation in neighborhoods lacking green space
   - Don't just create amenities for the already-privileged
   - The best place for a park is where there isn't one

### Phase 4: Implementation Strategy

**Phasing approach:**

| Phase | Scope | Timeline | Cost Range | Reversibility |
|-------|-------|----------|------------|---------------|
| **Quick win** | [What can be done immediately?] | [Days/weeks] | [Low] | [Tactical/temporary] |
| **Interim** | [What establishes the vision?] | [Months] | [Medium] | [Semi-permanent] |
| **Permanent** | [Full build-out] | [1-3 years] | [Full budget] | [Permanent] |

**Quick-win tactics (can implement immediately):**
- Paint (street murals, colorful crosswalks)
- Planters (movable, filled with greenery)
- Bollards (simple protection from cars)
- Tables and chairs (movable seating)
- Programming (events, markets, activities)
- Tactical urbanplata (low-cost, temporary interventions)

**Key insight:** Start with tactical/temporary interventions. Let people experience the space. Build support. Then make it permanent.

### Phase 5: Political Strategy

**Identify stakeholders:**

| Stakeholder | Interest | Position | Approach |
|-------------|----------|----------|----------|
| **Supporters** | [Who benefits?] | Support | Mobilize, make visible |
| **Opponents** | [Who loses?] | Oppose | Acknowledge, don't negotiate core |
| **Neutral** | [Who is unaffected?] | Movable | Educate, show benefits |
| **Decision-makers** | [Who approves?] | Varies | Political cover, data |

**Build the coalition:**
- Identify the majority who will benefit (non-car-owners, parents, elderly, businesses, property owners)
- Make supporters visible and vocal
- Frame as democratic equity, not anti-car

**Handle opposition:**

| Opposition Argument | Response |
|---------------------|----------|
| "Where will people park?" | Parking is a private need that has been subsidized at public expense; alternatives exist (transit, walking, cycling, remote parking) |
| "It will hurt businesses" | Evidence shows pedestrian spaces increase retail sales; cars don't shop, people do |
| "You're anti-car" | We're pro-people; cars remain an option but shouldn't dominate public space |
| "Traffic will get worse" | Induced demand means more road space = more traffic; less road space = people find alternatives |
| "It's not the right time" | There will never be a "right time"; opposition will exist whenever change is proposed |

**Political cover:**
- Cite successful examples from other cities
- Start with pilot/temporary to demonstrate success
- Measure and publicize benefits
- Let elected officials claim credit

## Error Handling

| Situation | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| Space is privately owned | Note ownership; recommend public acquisition or partnership; principles still apply |
| Strong political opposition | Recommend tactical/temporary approach to demonstrate success; build support gradually |
| Technical constraints (utilities, etc.) | Incorporate into design; rarely insurmountable |
| Budget constraints | Prioritize phases; start with quick wins; seek grants and partnerships |
| No clear community support | More engagement needed before proceeding; don't impose from above |
